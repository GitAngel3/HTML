A wildlife sanctuary

The Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972 is a landmark Indian law enacted to provide protection and conservation for wild animals, birds, and plants, as well as for matters connected to them. Key objectives include prohibiting hunting, poaching, and trading of endangered species, establishing penalties for these violations, and creating designated protected areas like National Parks and Wildlife Sanctuaries to preserve habitats and prevent wildlife exploitation.

Wildlife sanctuaries in India

Wildlife sanctuaries of India, are classified as IUCN Category IV protected areas. As of March 2025, 573 wildlife sanctuaries have been established, covering 123,762.56 km2 (47,784.99 sq mi).Among these, Project Tiger governs 53 tiger reserves, which are of special significance for the conservation of the Bengal tiger.Additionally, there are 33 elephant reserves covering 80,778 km2 (31,189 sq mi) established under the Project Elephant, some of which overlap with the boundaries of declared wildlife sanctuaries and tiger reserves.

      8. Animals facing extinction
      Nearly extinct animals like the Amur leopard, Javan and Sumatran rhinos, Mountain Gorillas, Black Rhinos, Sunda Island Tiger, and Lion-Tailed Macaque are protected in wildlife sanctuaries, which serve as critical havens to prevent their complete extinction by offering habitat protection and prohibiting hunting. For example, the Amur leopard is critically endangered in parts of Russia and China, while Indian sanctuaries protect rhinos and the Lion-Tailed Macaque
